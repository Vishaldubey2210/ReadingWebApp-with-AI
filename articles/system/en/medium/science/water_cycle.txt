Water is one of the most important substances on Earth, and it is always moving. The journey that water takes from the Earth's surface to the sky and back again is called the water cycle. This cycle has no beginning and no end. It keeps repeating forever, and without it, life on Earth would not be possible.

The water cycle begins with evaporation. When the Sun heats the surface of oceans, lakes, and rivers, water turns into water vapor and rises into the air. This process is called evaporation. Plants also release water vapor through their leaves in a process called transpiration. Together, evaporation and transpiration add enormous amounts of moisture to the atmosphere every single day.

As water vapor rises higher into the atmosphere, the air becomes cooler. Cool air cannot hold as much water vapor as warm air, so the vapor begins to condense, meaning it turns back into tiny droplets of liquid water. These droplets collect around tiny particles of dust and salt in the air, forming clouds. This stage of the water cycle is called condensation.

When clouds collect enough water droplets, the droplets combine and grow heavier. Eventually, they become too heavy to stay in the cloud, and they fall back to Earth. This is called precipitation. Precipitation can take many forms, such as rain, snow, sleet, or hail, depending on the temperature of the air. Rain falls in warm weather, while snow falls when the air is very cold.

When precipitation reaches the ground, it can do several things. Some water soaks into the soil and becomes groundwater. Plants absorb some of this groundwater through their roots. Some water flows across the surface of the land and collects in rivers, lakes, and streams. This surface flow is called runoff. Eventually, all this water finds its way back to the oceans, and the cycle starts all over again.

The water cycle is driven mainly by the energy of the Sun and the force of gravity. The Sun provides the heat needed for evaporation, and gravity pulls precipitation back down to Earth. Scientists believe that the water you drink today may have once been drunk by a dinosaur millions of years ago, because water has been cycling through the Earth's systems for billions of years.

Have you ever wondered how the food you eat gives you the energy to run, think, and grow? The answer lies in a process called digestion, which happens inside your body every time you eat a meal. Digestion is the process by which your body breaks down food into tiny nutrients that your cells can use for energy, growth, and repair.

The digestive system is a long, winding pathway that begins at your mouth and ends at a small opening called the anus. If you were to stretch it out completely, the human digestive system would be nearly nine meters long. That is almost three times the height of a grown adult! Along this journey, food is broken down both physically and chemically.

Digestion begins the moment you take a bite of food. Your teeth chew the food into smaller pieces, and your tongue moves it around to help break it down further. Your salivary glands release saliva into your mouth, which makes the food wet and slippery and easier to swallow. Saliva also contains a special chemical called an enzyme, which begins breaking down the starch in foods like bread and rice.

When you swallow, food travels down a tube called the esophagus, which connects your mouth to your stomach. The esophagus uses waves of muscle contractions to push food downward, so you can actually swallow even if you are hanging upside down. When food reaches your stomach, the real work of digestion begins.

Your stomach is a muscular, J-shaped organ that churns food around like a washing machine. It releases powerful acids and enzymes that break down proteins found in meat, eggs, and beans. After two to four hours of churning, the food becomes a thick liquid called chyme. This liquid is slowly released into the small intestine.

The small intestine is where most of the nutrients from your food are absorbed into your bloodstream. Despite being called small, this organ is actually about six meters long. It is coated with millions of tiny finger-like projections called villi, which increase the surface area available for absorbing nutrients. These nutrients then travel through your blood to every cell in your body.

After the small intestine has absorbed all the useful nutrients, the remaining material moves into the large intestine. Here, water is absorbed back into the body, and the leftover waste is compacted and eventually expelled. The entire journey from the time you eat your food to the time your body removes the waste usually takes between twenty-four and seventy-two hours.

Taking care of your digestive system is important. Eating a diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains, drinking plenty of water, and exercising regularly all help keep your digestive system healthy and working efficiently.

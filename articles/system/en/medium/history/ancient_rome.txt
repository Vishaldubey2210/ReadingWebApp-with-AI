More than two thousand years ago, one of the greatest empires the world has ever seen began in a small city in Italy. The Roman Empire grew from the city of Rome to control lands stretching from Britain in the north to Egypt in the south, and from Spain in the west to the borders of Persia in the east. At its height, the Roman Empire ruled over more than fifty million people and lasted for nearly five hundred years.

Rome started as a small kingdom on the banks of the Tiber River around 753 BCE. According to Roman legend, the city was founded by twin brothers named Romulus and Remus, who were raised by a wolf. Whether or not this legend is true, historians know that Rome grew quickly and became a powerful republic, governed by elected officials called senators. The Roman Republic expanded its territory through a series of wars, eventually defeating the powerful city of Carthage in North Africa and taking control of Greece.

The Roman Republic eventually became an empire when a general named Julius Caesar rose to power. After Caesar was assassinated by senators who feared he had too much power, his adopted son Octavian took control. Octavian became the first Roman Emperor and was given the title Augustus, meaning the respected one. Under Augustus, Rome entered a long period of peace and prosperity known as the Pax Romana, or Roman Peace, which lasted for over two hundred years.

The Romans were extraordinary builders and engineers. They constructed thousands of kilometers of roads that connected every part of their empire. These roads were so well built that some can still be seen in Europe today. The Romans also built aqueducts, which were channels that carried fresh water from distant mountains into cities. They invented concrete and used it to construct magnificent buildings, bridges, and the famous Colosseum in Rome, where gladiators fought in front of crowds of fifty thousand people.

Roman culture spread throughout the empire and had a lasting influence on European civilization. The Latin language spoken by the Romans became the foundation for modern languages such as French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, and Romanian. Roman law formed the basis of legal systems used in many countries today. Even the calendar we use, with its months and the concept of leap years, was developed by the Romans.

The Roman Empire eventually declined due to a combination of factors, including invasions by Germanic tribes, economic troubles, and political corruption. In 476 CE, the last Roman Emperor in the west was overthrown, marking what historians traditionally consider the end of the Western Roman Empire. However, the Eastern Roman Empire, known as the Byzantine Empire, continued for another thousand years. The legacy of Rome lives on in our laws, languages, architecture, and the way modern civilization is organized.

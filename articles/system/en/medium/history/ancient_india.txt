India is one of the oldest civilizations in the world, and its history is a fascinating story of ancient empires, great thinkers, powerful rulers, and the long struggle for freedom. From the banks of the Indus River to the plains of the Ganges, Indian civilization has been shaping the world for more than five thousand years.

The story of ancient India begins with the Indus Valley Civilization, which flourished between 2600 BCE and 1900 BCE. The people of this civilization built remarkably advanced cities such as Mohenjo-daro and Harappa, located in what is now Pakistan and northwestern India. These cities had planned streets, brick houses, and even underground drainage systems, which was extraordinary for that time. The Indus Valley people were skilled craftspeople and traders who exchanged goods with civilizations as far away as Mesopotamia in modern-day Iraq.

Around 1500 BCE, a group of people called the Aryans migrated into northern India from Central Asia. They brought with them a language called Sanskrit, which became the foundation for many Indian languages. The Aryans composed sacred texts called the Vedas, which are among the oldest religious writings in the world. The ideas in the Vedas formed the basis of Hinduism, one of the world's oldest religions still practiced today.

One of India's greatest empires was the Maurya Empire, which was founded by Chandragupta Maurya around 321 BCE. The most famous Mauryan emperor was Ashoka, who ruled from about 268 BCE to 232 BCE. After winning a brutal war, Ashoka was deeply troubled by the suffering he had caused and converted to Buddhism. He then dedicated his life to promoting peace, compassion, and good governance. He built hospitals and roads, dug wells for travelers, and sent Buddhist missionaries to spread the religion across Asia.

Another great empire was the Gupta Empire, which lasted from about 320 CE to 550 CE and is often called a golden age of Indian history. During this period, Indian scholars and mathematicians made remarkable advances. The mathematician Aryabhata calculated the value of pi and correctly explained that the Earth rotates on its axis and revolves around the Sun. Indian mathematicians also invented the decimal number system and the concept of zero, which spread to the Arab world and eventually to Europe, forming the basis of mathematics used everywhere today.

India also gave the world two major religions: Hinduism and Buddhism. Buddhism was founded by Siddhartha Gautama, who lived in India around the fifth century BCE. He taught that suffering could be overcome by following a path of wisdom, compassion, and meditation. Buddhism spread across Asia and eventually became one of the world's major religions.

In the sixteenth century, the Mughal Empire was established in India by Babur, a descendant of both Timur and Genghis Khan. The Mughals built magnificent monuments, the most famous of which is the Taj Mahal, constructed by Emperor Shah Jahan as a memorial to his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal. The Taj Mahal, made of white marble and decorated with precious stones, is considered one of the finest examples of architecture in the world.

The story of modern India is largely the story of freedom. For nearly two hundred years, India was ruled by the British Empire. In the twentieth century, a remarkable leader named Mahatma Gandhi led a peaceful movement for independence. Gandhi believed in nonviolence, or ahimsa, and taught Indians to resist British rule through peaceful protests, strikes, and civil disobedience rather than through violence. His methods inspired freedom movements around the world. Finally, on August 15, 1947, India gained its independence and became a free nation. Today, India is the world's largest democracy, home to over one billion four hundred million people and thousands of years of living history.
